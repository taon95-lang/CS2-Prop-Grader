Algorithmic Engineering and Quantitative Analysis for Professional Counter-Strike 2 Player Proposition GradingThe professional landscape of Counter-Strike 2 (CS2) wagering has transitioned from a market driven by qualitative observation to a high-frequency, data-centric environment where the identification of positive expected value (EV+) is contingent upon the synthesis of advanced statistical modeling, real-time data ingestion, and structural game theory. To construct a premier bot for grading player propositions—specifically focusing on kills, headshots, and individual performance metrics—one must move beyond the superficial tracking of historical averages. The objective is the creation of a system capable of calculating "fair odds" through the lens of a 100% market, thereby revealing the inefficiencies embedded in the "vig-heavy" lines offered by retail sportsbooks. This report details the architectural requirements for such a system, encompassing the mathematical foundations of expected value, the application of discrete probability distributions to player counts, the impact of the Max Rounds 12 (MR12) format on statistical volume, and the influence of environmental variables such as map-side bias and the LAN-to-Online performance discrepancy.The Quantitative Framework of Expected Value in Esports MarketsAt the core of professional prop grading lies the concept of Expected Value (EV), a statistical measure that quantifies the average outcome of a wager if it were to be repeated many times under identical conditions. For a bot to be classified as world-class, it must identify opportunities where the probability of a specific outcome, such as a player exceeding a kill threshold, is higher than the probability implied by the bookmaker's odds. This discrepancy, often termed the "edge," is the primary driver of long-term profitability.The mathematical formula for determining expected value integrates the probability of winning, the net payout, and the probability of losing against the initial stake :$$EV = (P_{win} \times \text{Payout}) - (P_{loss} \times \text{Stake})$$In this paradigm, a positive result (EV+) indicates a mathematically favorable bet, while a negative result (EV-) signals an eventually ruinous strategy. This de-viggered probability represents the market’s consensus on the true likelihood of the outcome, providing the most accurate signal for determining where a "soft" bookmaker may have mispriced their line.Statistical Modeling and Discrete Probability DistributionsDeciding whether a player will go over or under a specific line requires a more nuanced approach than calculating a simple mean. Player performance in CS2 is highly sensitive to the discrete nature of the game; a player cannot achieve half a kill, making the use of discrete probability distributions essential for accurate grading. The Poisson distribution is the industry standard for modeling the occurrence of events in a fixed interval, provided those events occur at a constant average rate and independently of one another.The Poisson Mechanism in Kill ProjectionsIn the context of CS2 props, the rate parameter, $\lambda$ (lambda), is derived from a player's historical data, adjusted for the strength of the opponent and the map being played. The probability of a player securing exactly $k$ kills is defined by the following formula:$$P(X=k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$$Where $e$ is Euler's number (approximately 2.71828) and $k!$ is the factorial of the target kill count. By summing the probabilities for all values below or above the sportsbook's line, the bot can generate a theoretical win percentage for the Over and Under positions.However, the standard Poisson model assumes that the mean and variance of the distribution are equal. In CS2, this assumption is frequently violated due to "overdispersion," where high-impact players exhibit greater variance (high-kill "pop-off" games or low-kill droughts) than a standard Poisson process would predict. This change fundamentally altered the statistical "ceiling" for player props. With fewer rounds to play, a team that loses both pistol rounds and the subsequent "follow-up" rounds has a diminished capacity to recover, often leading to "blowout" maps where the losing team’s star players have fewer opportunities to engage in gunfights. If the veto results in a "decider" map where a player has a sub-1.00 rating over the last 12 months, the "Under" on their kill prop becomes significantly more attractive.The LAN vs. Online Performance GapThe physical setting of a match is a major determinant of player performance. Professional CS2 is played both online (from home or team houses) and on LAN (at large-scale events like IEM or Majors). The primary technical difference is latency; on LAN, ping is effectively zero, which virtually eliminates "peeker’s advantage"—a phenomenon where the player moving around a corner sees their stationary opponent slightly before the opponent sees them.In online play, aggressive players (often called "onliners") can exploit this advantage to secure higher kill counts. Conversely, on LAN, defensive players holding static angles (particularly AWPers) regain their efficiency because the server lag does not punish their stationary position. Furthermore, LAN tournaments introduce immense psychological pressure from live crowds, which can cause "choking" or performance drops in less-seasoned players. A world-class bot must track a player’s "LAN vs. Online" rating discrepancy to avoid overvaluing a player’s online stats when they are playing on the big stage.Market Dynamics: Identifying Inefficiencies Through Sharp BenchmarkingTo determine the best edge, the bot must differentiate between "sharp" and "soft" markets. Sharp sportsbooks, most notably Pinnacle, serve as the "market makers" for the industry. They feature high limits and allow professional bettors to "shape" the lines through their wagers. Soft books, such as Bovada or retail US-based apps, are often slower to adjust their lines following sharp action.Line Shopping and Closing Line Value (CLV)The most effective strategy for identifying EV+ is "line shopping"—comparing the real-time movement at sharp books against the stagnant lines at soft books. If a player’s kill line at Pinnacle moves from 17.5 (-115) to 18.5 (+110) within minutes, it signals that "sharp money" has entered the market on the Over. If a bot identifies that a soft book is still offering the player at 17.5 (-110), it has found a high-value edge.The ultimate test of a bot’s proficiency is its Closing Line Value (CLV). CLV is the difference between the price at which the bet was placed and the final "fair" price at match start. The Kelly Criterion is the fundamental mathematical approach for determining how much of a bankroll to risk on a single prop based on the calculated edge.The bot should calculate the optimal bet fraction, $f^*$, using:$$f^* = \frac{bp - q}{b}$$Where $b$ is the decimal odds minus 1, $p$ is the model's win probability, and $q$ is the loss probability.However, because probability estimates in sports are rarely 100% precise (unlike a deck of cards), professional bettors use a "Fractional Kelly" approach. A 1/4 or 1/8 Kelly strategy reduces the volatility and protects the bankroll against "black swan" events, such as a player suffering a sudden technical issue or an unexpected medical emergency during a match.The Integrated Grading RubricA final requirement for the bot is a grading system that simplifies complex quantitative data into actionable signals for the user or the automated execution layer.
